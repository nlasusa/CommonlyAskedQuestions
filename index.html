Commonly Asked Questions
WHAT IS A SECURITIES FRAUD CLASS ACTION?
A securities fraud class action is a representative lawsuit brought on behalf of a group of investors who have suffered a financial loss in a particular security. Securities fraud class actions are civil lawsuits meaning that a person or entity (the Plaintiff) can seek to hold another person or entity (the Defendant) liable for violating the federal securities laws with the goal to provide monetary compensation to the investors (the Class) who were harmed during the relevant period (the Class Period). This differs from a criminal case, in which a prosecutor acts on behalf of the government. In a criminal case the goal is to punish the wrongdoer with imprisonment or fines for breaking the law. In a typical securities fraud class action, the financial loss is usually the result of fraudulent stock manipulation or materially false statements made to investors, frequently in SEC filings (10-K, 10-Q, proxy statements, etc.), earnings announcements and other investor presentations. Once the fraud is revealed, the company’s previously artificially inflated stock price or other security loses a significant amount of its value, resulting in investor losses.
HOW LONG DO THESE CASES TYPICALLY LAST?
Securities fraud class action cases usually take around 2 to 4 years to be resolved, although each case is different and this time frame could be longer or shorter.
WHAT ARE THE IMPORTANT EVENTS DURING A CASE?
1. THE COMPLAINT IS FILED
A securities fraud class action begins by filing a Civil Complaint. A Civil Complaint is a legal document that initiates a lawsuit by a Plaintiff and informs the persons and/or entities being sued, the Defendants, of the claims brought against them. The Complaint, among other things, lays out the facts and reasons that support the Plaintiff’s claims against the Defendants.

2. THE APPOINTMENT OF LEAD PLAINTIFF AND LEAD COUNSEL
In a securities fraud class action, the first event is the Court’s appointment of the Lead Plaintiff and Lead Counsel. The Plaintiff who files the first Complaint is not entitled to be the “Lead Plaintiff” simply by filing a Complaint. The Lead Plaintiff is the representative party who acts on behalf of all investor Class members in directing the litigation and is usually the investor or small group of investors who have the largest financial loss in the securities at issue during the Class Period. The Lead Plaintiff’s selection of counsel to represent the Lead Plaintiff and the Class, if approved by the Court, are Lead or Class Counsel.

3. THE AMENDED COMPLAINT IS FILED
After the leadership appointment, the Court will set a schedule for the Lead Plaintiff to file a detailed Amended Complaint. Typically, the deadline for this filing is approximately 60 days after the Court appoints the Lead Plaintiff and Lead Counsel. The Amended Complaint is usually much more detailed than the initial Complaint and frequently contains information provided by confidential witnesses as a result of Lead Counsel’s investigation. 4. THE MOTION TO DISMISS
Defendants will usually file a Motion to Dismiss the Amended Complaint. This means the Defendants will file a document asking the Court to end the case. The Lead Plaintiff will file an opposition to the Defendants’ Motion to Dismiss, explaining why the Amended Complaint contains the requisite facts and information to permit the case to move forward. The Court may set a hearing for this Motion, or it may make a decision based upon the submissions by the parties. If the Court denies the Defendants’ Motion to Dismiss (which can take 9 – 12 months or longer), it means the Court has rejected the Defendants’ request to end the case and is allowing the case to go to the discovery phase. If the Court grants the Defendants’ request, it means the Court has agreed to the Defendants’ request to end the case. Frequently, however, the Court will permit the Lead Plaintiff to file a new Amended Complaint in order to cure any deficiencies identified by the Court in its opinion dismissing the case.

5. THE DISCOVERY PHASE AND CLASS CERTIFICATION
The discovery phase is the period of time (typically between 12-18 months) when the Lead Plaintiff and Defendants can request documents and other information (like deposition testimony) from each other. Each side will also engage one or more experts to provide detailed expert reports and testimony, usually concerning the amount of potential damages. One of the purposes of the discovery phase is to enable the Lead Plaintiff to gather evidence (documents and testimony) to support their claims at trial. During this time, the Lead Plaintiff will also file a Motion for Class Certification which, because a large number of people (the Class) have been injured by the same Defendants in the same way and during the same time period (the Class Period), asks the Court to enter an order declaring that the action should proceed to trial as a Class Action.

6. PRE-TRIAL MOTIONS AND TRIAL
After the conclusion of the discovery phase, the Court, if it already has not, will typically enter an order setting the deadlines for Pre-Trial motions and Trial. Many times the Court will allow the parties to file a Motion for Summary Judgment. If the Defendants file such a motion, they will ask the Court to end the case by arguing that the undisputed facts do not support a jury finding in favor of the Lead Plaintiff and the Class. The Court will also usually hear motions from the parties asking it not to permit certain evidence from being used at Trial. After that, the case will go to Trial where the jury will decide whether or not the Lead Plaintiff proved the claims by a preponderance of the evidence. This means that the Lead Plaintiff has the burden to prove to the fact finder (the jury) that there is a greater than 50% chance that the claims are true. It is important to note that it is extremely rare for securities class action lawsuits to go all the way through to a jury verdict.
